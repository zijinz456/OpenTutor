## Reddit Post — r/LocalLLaMA

**Title:** I built a cognitive load weight auto-tuner for my local AI tutor — no ML, just a 20-message sliding window

**Body:**

OpenTutor is an open-source, local-first AI learning platform — FastAPI backend, Next.js frontend, SQLite, runs on Ollama or any OpenAI-compatible endpoint. The tutor tracks cognitive load in real time using signals like error rate, message hesitation, correction frequency, and response length. Each signal is weighted and combined into a score that drives adaptive decisions: slowing down, switching to review mode, suggesting breaks, deferring new material.

In April I hit a calibration problem that I think is underappreciated in adaptive systems generally: signal saturation.

**The problem**

A student grinding through hard material might sit at error_rate = 0.8 for 20 consecutive messages. That's not a transient overload spike — that's their baseline for this topic. But the cognitive load scorer sees a sustained high signal and keeps firing "overload" responses at full intensity. After message 20, the system has stopped adapting. It's just saturated on a state that's become normal for this student in this context.

The root cause: the signal weights are global defaults. They assume every student has the same baseline. They don't.

**The fix: sliding window dampening**

`cognitive_load_tuning.py`, 136 lines. Design is intentionally simple:

- A per-user sliding window of length 20 tracks each signal's recent values
- After 5+ data points, if the rolling mean exceeds 0.65 (persistently high), the signal's weight multiplier is decayed by a factor of 0.90 per reading
- Weight floor is 0.40 — a signal can be suppressed by at most 60% of its original weight, never zeroed out
- When the mean drops below threshold, the weight slowly recovers at 1.04x per reading
- In-memory only, LRU-style eviction at 200 users, no database writes

The constants live at the top of the file (`WINDOW_SIZE`, `HIGH_SIGNAL_THRESHOLD`, `WEIGHT_DECAY_FACTOR`, etc.) and are tunable without touching the logic.

**Why not ML?**

This is an explicit Phase 1 decision. The docstring says: "Phase 2 can add Bayesian updating if needed." I ruled out a model because the weights are essentially priors — and for short sessions (most OpenTutor sessions are under an hour) you don't have enough per-user data to trust a posterior. More importantly: in an educational context, "the model decided" is not an acceptable answer when a student asks why the tutor kept suggesting they take a break. The sliding window is inspectable. You can print the multipliers and understand exactly which signals are dampened and why.

**The block unlock overlay (same commit)**

OpenTutor has 12 composable learning blocks that unlock progressively based on learning behavior. Before this change, locked blocks were just absent — you couldn't see what you were missing or how to get there. Now they render with a backdrop-blur overlay, a lock icon, the block name, and the specific unlock condition. The block content is visible underneath the blur but not interactive. Borrowed from game design: showing the gate makes the unlock feel like a goal.

**The ambient content proposal (same commit)**

The background ambient monitor gained a new Tier 2 action: `propose_content_generation`. Unlike previous actions (which execute automatically), this creates a pending approval task — the student sees a card saying "I think you'd benefit from AI-generated notes for this course" and has to say yes before anything happens. The monitor includes a dedup check: if an identical pending or running task already exists, it skips. This is the first action in the monitor that's pure proposal rather than execution.

**The honest admission**

The constants I picked — 0.65, 0.90, 0.40, window=20 — were educated guesses. I tuned them against a handful of test sessions before shipping, not against real user data. With a larger user base I'd run proper ablations on these values. For now, the system is shipping a heuristic that I can explain and observe, and I'm collecting the data to eventually justify something more sophisticated.

If you've built adaptive signal calibration for educational tech — or anything with streaming behavioral signals — I'd be curious how you approached baseline detection. OpenTutor repo: https://github.com/zijinz456/opentutor

What's the largest gap between the "right" solution and what you actually shipped?

## Reddit Post — r/Anki

**Title:** I added cognitive load auto-calibration to my FSRS-based AI tutor — the signal saturation problem FSRS already solves that cognitive tracking doesn't

**Body:**

I maintain OpenTutor, an open-source AI learning platform built around FSRS 4.5 (same algorithm as Anki since 23.10). One of the features is real-time cognitive load tracking during study sessions: error rate, response hesitation, correction frequency, message length patterns. The system uses the combined score to modulate pacing, trigger review, and decide when to introduce new material.

In April I ran into a problem that I think FSRS handles elegantly on the scheduling side, but that cognitive load tracking doesn't have an equivalent answer to: baseline calibration.

**The saturation problem**

A student drilling hard cards might have error_rate = 0.8 for their first 20 minutes with a topic. That's not acute overload — that's their baseline for this material at this stage of learning. FSRS accounts for this: the algorithm tracks your individual retention curve per card, and a card with a long history of hard reviews will have a very short target interval. The signal (difficulty) informs the schedule.

Cognitive load tracking doesn't have that individual history. The weights are global. So error_rate = 0.8 means "high load" regardless of whether that's your baseline or a spike. The system kept firing overload responses — slow down, review, break — for someone who was just in a hard stretch, handling it.

**The fix**

`cognitive_load_tuning.py`, 136 lines. A sliding window of the last 20 readings per signal. If the rolling mean stays above 0.65 for at least 5 readings, the signal's weight decays by 10% per reading, down to a floor of 40% of its original weight. When the mean drops, the weight recovers at 4% per reading.

The interaction with FSRS: the two systems are intentionally decoupled. FSRS decides when to show a card based on your historical retention data. Cognitive load tuning decides how the tutor should respond in real time as you're working on a card — and that response is now calibrated to your recent session pattern, not just the global defaults.

**Why this is similar to FSRS stability**

FSRS's stability parameter tracks how durable a memory trace is. A high-stability card needs a long interval. A low-stability card needs a short one. The system adapts per card over time.

The cognitive load tuner does something analogous at the signal level: a signal that's been persistently high for this user in this session is treated as "stable high" — a known state rather than an alert. Its influence on the adaptive score is reduced. The system stops reacting to information it's already incorporated.

This is Phase 1. The docstring explicitly notes "Phase 2 can add Bayesian updating if needed." A proper Bayesian approach would be closer to what FSRS does — maintain a prior per user per signal, update on every session. The problem is the same as early FSRS use: you need enough per-user data before the posterior is trustworthy. Most OpenTutor sessions are short. Phase 1 is a heuristic.

**What else shipped at the same time**

The 12 composable learning blocks now show a locked overlay when their unlock conditions aren't met. Before: locked blocks were absent. Now: you can see them (blurred), you can see the lock, and you can read the unlock condition. This is a small UX thing but meaningfully different — the block becomes a visible goal rather than an unknown.

OpenTutor is fully local — Ollama by default, or any OpenAI-compatible endpoint. Full repo: https://github.com/zijinz456/opentutor

If you've used FSRS outside of Anki — in a custom system, a study tracker, a language app — how have you handled the cold-start period before you have enough data to trust the algorithm's estimates?

## IndieHackers Post

**Title:** My AI tutor had a calibration bug I didn't want to fix the "right" way. So I shipped 136 lines instead.

**Body:**

OpenTutor is an open-source, local-first AI learning platform I've been building for about a year now. It runs entirely on your own hardware — Ollama, or any OpenAI-compatible endpoint. One of its distinguishing features is real-time cognitive load tracking: the tutor watches your behavior during a session and adapts in response.

For months, it had a problem I knew about and kept avoiding.

**The problem**

Cognitive load is computed from signals: error rate, hesitation, correction frequency, response length patterns. Each signal has a weight, and the combined score drives how the tutor adapts. The weights are global defaults — the same for every student.

A student who's been working through hard material for 20 minutes might have an error rate of 0.8 the whole time. That's not a crisis — that's their baseline for this topic right now. But the scorer sees error_rate=0.8 and treats it identically to someone who just slammed into a wall. Every overload response fires at full intensity: slow down, suggest a break, defer new material. Over and over. By message 20, the system has stopped adapting to the student. It's looping on a state that's become their normal.

**Why I kept putting off the fix**

The right fix, in my head, was a per-user baseline model. Bayesian priors learned from session history, updated with each recording. Something with actual statistical grounding that I could defend.

That kept being a project I'd start later, after I had more users, after I had more data. The better solution always felt just out of reach.

**What I actually shipped in April**

`cognitive_load_tuning.py`. 136 lines.

A sliding window of the last 20 signal readings per user. If the rolling mean for a signal stays above 0.65 for at least 5 readings, its weight multiplier decays by 10%. The floor is 0.40 — you can never suppress a signal below 40% of its original weight. When the mean drops, the weight recovers slowly, at 4% per reading. The whole thing runs in memory, no database writes, capped at 200 users.

The docstring says: "Phase 2 can add Bayesian updating if needed." That's the honest version of the plan. Phase 1 is a heuristic I can explain to anyone who asks. If a student complains "why did the tutor keep telling me to slow down?", I can say: "Because your error rate was above 0.65 for 15 messages, and the system was treating that as baseline overload. The weight should have dampened sooner. Here are the multiplier values." I can show the state. I couldn't do that with a model.

**What I didn't know would happen**

The sliding window also catches recovery. When a student finishes a hard stretch and their error rate drops, the suppressed signal's weight climbs back toward 1.0. The tuner isn't just dampening — it's adapting in both directions. I didn't think about that explicitly when I wrote it, but it's how the math works out.

**The rest of the commit**

Two other things landed in the same April 2nd push.

Locked block overlays: OpenTutor has 12 learning blocks that unlock progressively. Before, locked blocks were just gone — you had no way to know they existed or how to get them. Now they render under a blur with a lock icon and the unlock condition spelled out. "Complete 5 flashcard reviews to unlock quiz mode." Small thing. The difference in feel is large.

The ambient monitor's first "Tier 2" action: proposing content generation instead of executing it. The background process that watches session state can now decide "this student probably needs AI-generated notes" and surface a pending approval card rather than generating things silently. The student has to explicitly say yes. I added this because silent generation felt wrong once I saw it in practice — the tutor should propose, not act.

**The thing I'm still uncertain about**

The constants: 0.65, 0.90, 0.40, window=20. I picked these with a small number of test sessions, not rigorous data. I've watched them in production since April and haven't seen anything that made me immediately regret the choices. But I'd calibrate them differently if I were starting from real usage data.

I went from "I need an ML model to do this right" to "I shipped 136 lines of math" in about a day. The heuristic works well enough to ship, and now I have months of data to eventually justify something better.

OpenTutor: https://github.com/zijinz456/opentutor
